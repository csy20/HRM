1.1 What is HRM?

Definition

"HRM is the strategic approach to the effective management of people in a company such that they help their business gain a competitive advantage."

It involves hiring, developing, and retaining the right talent. Unlike traditional administration, HRM views employees as **investments** (Human Capital) rather than expenses.

1.2 PM vs HRM

Exam Favorite
Basis Personnel Management (PM) Human Resource Management (HRM)
Nature Routine, Administrative, Maintenance-oriented. Strategic, Continuous, Growth-oriented.
Focus Managing people as tools/cost. Managing people as assets/partners.
Decision Making Slow, bureaucratic, top-down. Fast, decentralized, participative.
Time Horizon Short-term. Long-term.
Treatment Employees are treated as a cost variable. Employees are treated as a resource/capital.

1.3 Challenges in HRM

  • Globalization: Managing a workforce across different time zones, cultures, and legal systems. (e.g., An Indian manager leading a German team).
  • Workforce Diversity: Integrating different ages (Gen Z vs Boomers), genders, and ethnicities into a cohesive team. The challenge is ensuring inclusivity.
  • Technological Shifts: Adapting to AI in recruitment, remote work tools, and automation which might make some skills obsolete.
  • Work-Life Balance: Preventing burnout in a 24/7 connected digital economy.

2.1 HRIS (HR Information System)

CSBS Special

Definition: Software or online solution for the data entry, data tracking, and data information needs of the HR function.

Key Functions:

  • Operational: Payroll processing, attendance tracking, leave management.
  • Strategic (DSS): Analyzing attrition trends, predicting future hiring needs using data analytics.
  • Self-Service: Portals where employees can update their own address, bank details, etc.

2.2 HR Audit

Systematic verification of HR practices.

  • Compliance: Are we following labor laws?
  • Efficiency: Is our hiring process too slow?
  • Output: Audit Report recommending changes.

2.3 HR Accounting

Quantifying human resources in financial terms.

  • Cost Model: Historical cost of hiring + training.
  • Value Model: Present value of future earnings generated by the employee.

3.4 Appraisal (MBO)

Key

Management by Objectives (MBO):

  1. Set organizational goals.
  2. Set individual goals (Jointly with employee).
  3. Monitor progress continuously.
  4. Evaluate & Reward.

Benefits: Clarity & Motivation.

4.1 HRP Process Steps

"Ensuring the right people, at the right place, at the right time."

Step 1: Demand Forecasting

Estimating future need for employees.

  • Trend Analysis: Looking at past hiring ratios.
  • Managerial Judgment: Asking dept heads what they need.

Step 2: Supply Forecasting

Checking current inventory.

  • Skill Inventory: Database of current staff skills.
  • Markov Analysis: Statistical model tracking movement (promotions/exits).

Step 3: Gap Analysis

Surplus (Too many) Action: Layoffs, VRS, Reduced hours.
Shortage (Too few) Action: Recruitment, Overtime, Subcontracting.

5.1 Strategic HRM (SHRM)

SHRM is linking HR policies with the strategic goals of the organization to improve business performance.

Strategy: Cost Leadership

Goal: Be the cheapest.

HR Action: Hire flexible/temp workers, focus on efficiency training, pay based on output.

Strategy: Differentiation/Innovation

Goal: Be unique.

HR Action: Hire creative talent, reward risk-taking, long-term development.

5.2 Service Sector HRM

Vital for CSBS
  • The "Moment of Truth": In services (like IT consulting), production and consumption happen simultaneously. The interaction between the employee and client determines quality.
  • Emotional Labor: Employees must manage their emotions to create a public facial and bodily display (e.g., a helpdesk agent staying calm with an angry user). HR must hire for "Emotional Intelligence" (EQ).
  • Service Recovery: The ability to fix a mistake immediately. Requires Empowerment‚Äîgiving frontline staff the authority to solve problems without asking a manager (e.g., waiving a fee).
